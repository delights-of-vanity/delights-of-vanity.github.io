## The Composer

Sergei Prokofiev (1891–1953) was a Russian composer and pianist of extraordinary range — capable of icy modernism, biting wit, lyrical warmth, and sardonic humor, sometimes all within the same piece. He studied at the St. Petersburg Conservatory where he scandalized audiences with his early dissonant works, then spent years in the West before returning to the Soviet Union in 1936, where he spent the rest of his life navigating the demands of Soviet cultural policy. His output includes operas, ballets (*Romeo and Juliet*, *Cinderella*), piano concertos, and seven symphonies.

## The Piece

The Symphony No. 1 in D major, Op. 25 — nicknamed *"Classical"* by Prokofiev himself — was composed between 1916 and 1917. His intention was to write the kind of symphony Haydn might have written had he lived into the 20th century: compact, clear, elegantly structured — but with a modern harmonic edge that constantly surprises.

It succeeds brilliantly. The first movement crackles with energy; the second is a slow, almost teasing lyricism; the third is a *Gavotta* — a deliberately archaic dance form — given a fresh, slightly absurdist treatment; and the finale races to a bright conclusion. The whole thing lasts barely fifteen minutes and leaves you smiling.

It was one of the earliest neoclassical symphonies, predating Stravinsky's more famous neoclassical turn, and remains one of the most purely enjoyable pieces in the orchestral repertoire.
