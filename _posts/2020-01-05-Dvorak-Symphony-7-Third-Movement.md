## The Composer

Antonín Dvořák (1841–1904) was a Czech composer who brought the folk music and landscapes of Bohemia onto the world stage without ever becoming merely folksy. He had a gift for melody that felt both immediate and inevitable — tunes that sound like they could not have been written any other way. Alongside Brahms (who championed him) and Tchaikovsky, he was one of the dominant symphonic voices of the late Romantic era. Today he is best known for the *New World Symphony*, but his Seventh is perhaps his greatest.

## The Piece

The Symphony No. 7 in D minor, Op. 70 (1885) is widely considered Dvořák's most serious and dramatically concentrated symphony. He wrote it under the inspiration of Brahms's Third and the pressure of wanting to create "a work that would shake the world." It largely succeeds.

The **third movement** — Scherzo: Vivace — is the emotional heart of the symphony. It bursts open with rhythmic energy rooted in the *furiant*, a traditional Bohemian dance with shifting accents that creates a driving, almost destabilizing momentum. The central trio section offers a moment of lyrical relief before the dance returns. It is music that feels alive and restless from the first bar.
