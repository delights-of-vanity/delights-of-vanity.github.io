## The Composer

Anton Bruckner (1824–1896) was an Austrian composer and organist whose vast, cathedral-like symphonies occupy a unique place in the Romantic repertoire. Deeply religious and chronically self-doubting, he revised his works obsessively and struggled for decades to gain recognition. His symphonies are long, slow-moving, built in great architecture blocks of sound — music that demands patience but rewards it with a sense of cosmic scale that few composers have matched. Wagner was his idol, and the influence shows in the grandeur and harmonic richness of his writing.

## The Piece

The Symphony No. 0 in D minor — *"die Nullte"* — was composed in 1863–64 and revised in 1869. Bruckner himself assigned it the number zero, declaring it *"invalid"* (*ungültig*) after he came to regard it as inadequate, though he never destroyed it. It was not performed publicly until 1924, nearly thirty years after his death.

The dismissal was characteristically harsh self-criticism. The symphony is a genuine and substantial work — already recognizably Bruckner in its broad phrases, its sudden silences, and its imposing brass writing. It lacks the supreme confidence of the later numbered symphonies, but that makes it fascinating rather than weak: you can hear the voice forming. The video below starts at a later movement.
